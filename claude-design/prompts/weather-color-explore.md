# Weather in color — open exploration (card-faces phase)

A **divergent color pass**, run as a follow-up in the same **Weather Eye** chat (so Fable keeps all the
Weather data, the glyphs, and the chosen Transit direction). The goal is to break out of the monochrome and
see what color does, deliberately *not* prescribing how. Whatever works here sets the tone for the whole
system; we reconcile the Clock / Weather / Linear designs afterward, then design Calendar + Claude usage in
the resulting palette. See [[aod-ui-redesign-pivot]].

Framing: don't tell it how. Give freedom + the two constraints that still hold (real data only; it still
lives on a 24/7 ambient wall). Ask for a range (subtle → bold) so we can find the level.

---

## The prompt (paste into the Weather Eye chat as a follow-up)

> A new direction, building on the Weather cards you just designed (Vela - Weather Eye).
>
> Stepping back across everything we've designed so far, the whole system has come out very **monochrome**:
> white and gray on near-black with one restrained accent. On a phone that reads as disciplined. Seen all
> together on the wall, though, it feels austere and a little cold, more like a screen than something alive.
> We think we've been too strict with ourselves.
>
> So: **be more creative, and bring in color.** We want the wall to feel warmer and more alive, less
> monochromatic. You have real latitude here, break the one-accent restraint we've been holding, use color
> with freedom and personality, and treat this as permission to make Weather genuinely beautiful, not just
> correct.
>
> What still holds (everything else is open):
> - **Only the real Weather data** we established: temperature, the condition (the eight kinds, day and
>   night), feels-like, humidity, wind, and the forecast (hi/lo, chance of rain, sunrise and sunset). Color
>   can come from expression or from that data itself, never from data we don't have.
> - **It still lives on a wall, on 24 hours a day, glanced from across a room** (and on a phone). So the color
>   should still be something a person is happy to have glowing in the room all day, alive but not tiring.
>   That's the real challenge: color that belongs on an always-on ambient surface, not a poster.
> - For reference, not obligation: the brand mark is a warm gold sail, and the Clock already warms to a
>   deep-red ember after dark. Riff on that, or find your own way.
> - Whatever works here will likely set the tone for the whole app, so lean toward color ideas that could
>   live across every card, not Weather-only tricks.
>
> Show us **a few directions** that explore different amounts and kinds of color, from a subtle warmth all the
> way to something boldly expressive, so we can see the range and find the right level. You're free to evolve or even rethink them if color
> takes you somewhere better. Keep it dark-first, on a phone and on the landscape wall, with the real Quito
> data, and include a night frame so we see how the color behaves after dark.
>
> Surprise us.
